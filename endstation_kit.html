1 Main Objectives

  1. Terminate photon delivery system with an appropriately sized Be window
  2. Gantry to support vacuum pressure of about 700 pounds on the Be window. This gantry should move vertically with the table to track the various beams.
  3. BPM precedes the Be window as part of an assembly of slits + BPM + window.
  4. The main table is something like 2.5 m by 1.5 m and has 4 degrees of freedom:
    • Vertical to follow beam height
    • Pitch to make surface of table parallel to beam
    • Yaw and horizontal to align table holes to beam path
  5. Rail on table supports fixed height ion chambers. Setting the table in pitch and vertical ensures that the ion chambers are in beam path. These supports will need to be engineered for the operating height table.
  6. Transport pipe for delivering beam to goniometer.

1.1 Be window

I am assuming that this is a thin window which is a bit bigger than the beam. It gets positioned vertically into the beam (with a bit of wiggle room for adjusting the mirrors).

1.2 Be window gantry

The gantry must support a vacuum force in the negative beam direction and adjust vertically accroding to the beam height.

Options:

  • Gantry is mounted on the main table, as main table moves to meet the beam, the window moves with it.
  • Gantry sits on an independent table whose motion is coordinated with the main table motion.

After the meeting on 9 Sep., we agreed that the independent table option makes more sense. This would be a table, independent of the XAFS table, which provides the support against the vacuum force on the window.

There would be a single vertical actuator to raise and lower the window assembly. This assembly would supported against the vacuum pressure on the window by curved rail system. As the actuator raises and lowers the window assembly, the pitch of the assembly would be set by a curved track. As the assembly is lowered, the track pitches the assembly downward such that components of the assembly are always perpendicular to the incoming beam.

Not shown in this crude drawing is the actual gantry that supports the window assembly against the vacuum pressure.

1.2.1 Window assembly

The Be window, the nanoBPM, and a set of hutch slits would be bolted together and attached to the beam transport pipe by a bellows. These three items would then be moved vertically to intercept the beam in each of the modes.

1.2.2 Connection to goniometer transport pipe

The assembly is terminated with a quick-connect flange for mating to the upstream end of the transport pipe.

A pivoting yoke is mounted (bolts or magnets) to the table supporting the window assembly. This yoke supports the upstream end of the transport pipe, but can be removed when not in use.

The transport pipe joins the quick-connect flange by a bellows (thus allowing the pitch of the transport pipe to be adjusted when changing beamline modes). The yoke must extend downstream, beyond the bellows, to support the pipe.

1.3 BPM

This is, presumably, FMBO's nanoBPM.

1.4 Table

See "market research" from Axilon

  • Optical table supported on a granite block.
  • Articulation of all degrees of freedom.
  • Need to define margins for vertical, pitch, horizontal, and roll. The horizontal motion must be enough to lower a "typical" XAFS experiment out of the way to allow placement of the goniometer transport pipe.
  • In many cases, table will not be level, so a rim will be needed around the downstream end of the table.

The "market research" from Axilon is substantively similar to the table supporting the ISS harmonic rejection mirror. Klaus has promised to share those specs with me. He tells me that the delivery time for his table (which is more complex than ours would be) is 9 months.

1.5 Rail system

The base of this can be standard X-rail. The vertical supports for both the ion chambers and the transport pipe need design work.

The ion chamber supports should be fixed. When the table is at a height and pitch appropriate to the mirror positions, the ion chambers will be in the beam.

The transport pipe support is a bit more complicated. It should have some flexibility in pitch such that, along with the bellows attaching it to the entry window, its is possible to clamp the bellows in place and adjust the height of the downstream support.

1.5.1 Downstream support

The downstream support for the transport pipe should be mounted on the goniometer table. This support can be fixed height, thus guaranteeing that the transport pipe is always pointed at the goniometer eucentric.

This support must be hinged to allow the pipe to change pitch when moving between modes.

1.6 Transport pipe

  • Pipe on some material. If heavy, it would be with fittings on the top side for lifting with the overhead hoist.
  • Supported at the upstream end by a hinged yoke mounted on the support for the window assembly gantry.
  • Supported at downstream end by fixed height pillar with a hinged yoke and mounted on the goniometer table.
  • Quick connect to upstream window flange via bellows. (So, not UHV)
  • Be or plastic window on downstream end.
  • Pumping port. Rough, maybe with turbo.
  • Will need clear procedure for placement of pipe at a specific angle and for adjustment of angle when changing modes.

2 Other features

Here is a list of additional toys, widgets, and features I would like to have for the end station.

2.1 Gas handling

System (possibly automated) for rough-pumping ion chambers and refilling with new gases. (Get details from, e.g., B18 at Diamond.)

May need new ion chambers.

2.2 Camera

See https://www.dectris.com/pilatus-100k.html#main_head_navigation. The Pilatus 100K is quite cheap, but not for long.

Talk to Jean about whether this is something IBM might purchase.

Many, many uses:

  • Beam profile characterization at arbitrary positions along beam path
  • Area detector for goniometer
  • Full-field XAFS
  • Yoneda-EXAFS
  • bulk-XES rig

2.3 Stages I'd like to have

  • Three 4-axis stacks (x,y,pitch,yaw)

    • 2 for 4-channel ion chambers
    • 1 for polycap lens

    I would like permanent mounting stages for these instruments so that they can become regular fixtures at the beamline.

  • Rotation stage for samples – disk bolted to rotation, all standing on a two-axis stage. A rotation stage with a disk can easily mount 20 or more sample. Translation between samples can be made without needing to move the fluorescence detector (the caveat is that there must be adequate space in front of the detector for the samples to pass by). My thought is to make many (50 or so) plates and loan them to visitors for mounting samples at home.
  • Rotation stage for reference foils – disk bolted to rotation, all standing on a two-axis stage. This allows a change of reference foil to be easily automated.
